### Failure Mode Analysis

k=0.006 is effective only for 1-tBu__CO2. For the other 10 reactions, it is insufficient to drive the reaction to completion. There are two different failure modes:

**Mode A: High-temperature runaway (small-system CO2 reactions)**

1-Me__CO2, 1-H__CO2, and 1-CN__CO2 start with large rti_dist values (8.3-9.6 A). Under the restraint pulling force, the systems move rapidly toward the target, but DeePMD PES generates repulsion. Kinetic energy accumulates and temperature rises sharply (peaks of 1237-1302 K). The thermostat (target 300 K, thermo_lambda ~0.999) cannot remove the heat in time, so the systems oscillate at high temperature instead of converging into the product basin.

Common features of these systems: small substituents (H, Me, CN), small total atom counts (24-27), and insufficient internal degrees of freedom to dissipate energy injected by the restraint.

**Mode B: Cold stagnation (large-substituent reactions)**

1-PMe2__CO2, 1-Ph__CO2, and 1-SiMe3__CO2 start with smaller rti_dist values (1.3-4.0 A), meaning the fragments in IS geometry are already relatively close to product. But the restraint force is too weak (k=0.006), so the systems hover near the starting region. Temperature remains low (195-341 K), and they lack enough kinetic energy to cross rearrangement barriers on the PES. The best bond-forming frames appear early (steps 110-124), after which the systems drift away.

**Mode B': Moderate heating (non-CO2 reactions in the 1-tBu series)**

1-tBu__CS2, 1-tBu__H2CO, 1-tBu__MeCN, and 1-tBu__MeCH=NMe start with intermediate rti_dist values (3.8-5.4 A). The restraint provides some pulling force and temperature rises moderately (335-509 K), but not enough to reach the product region. Final B-X and N-C distances remain far larger than reference product values.

### Why Only 1-tBu__CO2 Succeeded

The combined advantages of 1-tBu__CO2:
1. **Steric hindrance of the tBu substituent**: provides moderate spatial guidance and helps orient CO2 toward the B center
2. **Linear geometry of CO2**: two symmetrically placed O atoms increase the probability of B bond formation
3. **Appropriate IS initial distance**: rti_dist = 7.97 A, giving the restraint a sufficient "pulling lever arm"
4. **Large number of tBu degrees of freedom**: 36 atoms provide enough internal vibrational modes to dissipate excess kinetic energy

Failures in the other 1-tBu-series reactions show that **the electronic structure and geometry of the small molecule decisively affect the reaction path**:
- CS2: S atoms are larger, the B-S bond is longer, and k=0.006 is not enough to overcome steric repulsion from S
- H2CO: only one O atom, and H2CO is planar, so the orientation requirement is stricter
- MeCN/MeCH=NMe: B-N bond formation involves different electronic structure, and N-containing small molecules may compete with N2 for coordination in IS

### Reliability of the `promising` Flag

Only 3/11 reactions are marked `promising=True` (1-tBu__CO2, 1-Me__CO2, 1-CN__CO2), but the final B-X/N-C distances for 1-Me__CO2 and 1-CN__CO2 are still far from bond-forming thresholds. The basis of `promising_bond_threshold_met` needs to be rechecked; the threshold may be too loose, or the flag may use the best frame in the trajectory rather than the final frame.

#### Phase 1 Analysis

**Overall judgment: increasing k (0.01~0.05) did not produce a product state for any reaction within 1000 steps.** The only successful case remains 1-tBu__CO2 at k=0.006.

The three reaction categories respond very differently to increasing k:

**A. Small-system CO2 reactions (1-Me, 1-H, 1-CN): B-O can form, but temperature runs away**

- B-X distances can fall close to reference values (1.174~1.416 A), indicating that the B-O bond can form under strong restraint
- But temperature spikes: at k=0.05, 1-H__CO2 reaches 6749K, 1-CN__CO2 reaches 5234K, and 1-Me__CO2 reaches 4822K
- **These extreme temperatures mean kinetic energy far exceeds chemical bond energies (~3 eV ~= 35000K for C-C); the system is effectively undergoing nonphysical high-energy collision motion. A "short" B-X distance may be an artifact of restraint forcing and does not represent real chemical bond formation**
- N-C distances remain far above reference values (best case 1-CN k=0.05: N-C=1.221, but T=5234K is physically meaningless)

**B. Large-substituent CO2 reactions (1-PMe2, 1-Ph, 1-SiMe3): almost no response**

- Final B-X distances show very little difference among the three k values (for example 1-PMe2: 3.077 vs 3.500 vs 3.111)
- Temperature stays low (286~942K); restraint force is insufficient to overcome steric hindrance from large substituents
- Best bond-forming frames still have B-X/N-C distances far above references
- **Conclusion: k <= 0.05 is not enough for these systems; k >= 0.1 or a more fundamental strategy change is needed**

**C. Non-CO2 reactions in the 1-tBu series (H2CO, CS2, MeCN, MeCH=NMe): moderate response, H2CO is best**

## Code-Level Explanation of "1D Restraint"

### Why It Is Called "1D"

The RC-MD restraint consists of two independent distances (B-X and N-C), but **they share the same force constant k**. There is only one tunable knob, so it is called "1D".

The code is defined in [rcmd_lib.py:352-369](research/ic5c02384/scripts/rcmd_lib.py#L352-L369):

```python
def reaction_coordinate_restraints(target, config, k):
    # Restraint 1: N-C distance
    DistanceRestraint(n_atom, c_atom, n_c_dist, k, "N-C")
    # Restraint 2: B-X distance
    DistanceRestraint(b_atom, x_atom, b_x_dist, k, "B-X")
    # <- both restraints use the same k
```

The potential function ([bias.py:121-138](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/pes/bias.py#L121-L138)) is the sum of two independent harmonic oscillators:

```
E_bias = 1/2 k(d_BX - target_BX)^2 + 1/2 k(d_NC - target_NC)^2
```

The output `rti_dist` (`distance_column`) is the RMS of the two deviations:

```
rti_dist = sqrt((d_BX - target_BX)^2 + (d_NC - target_NC)^2)
```

### Limitations of 1D

Increasing k synchronously strengthens the pulling force along both B-X and N-C. But B-X bond formation (nucleophilic attack) and N-C bond formation (subsequent rearrangement) are two kinetically distinct steps. The 1D design cannot control them separately - if k is increased further to bring N-C closer, B-X has already been over-tightened, and the excess energy is released as heat, causing temperature runaway.

This motivates the "2D restraint" suggested above: give B-X and N-C independent k values and schedules, for example first drive B-X bond formation (large k_BX, small k_NC), then drive N-C bond formation.

## Dual-Track Bias in the JAX RTIPMD Version: RTIP Gaussian Bias vs RC-MD Harmonic Restraint

After comparing the JAX and Rust source code, I found that JAX `bias.py` actually contains two **completely independent** bias mechanisms, both dispatched through `md.py:254-263` and sharing the same MD engine (leapfrog + Berendsen thermostat):

```python
# md.py:254-263 _md_bias()
if isinstance(config, RepulsivePot):          # RTIP Gaussian repulsion
if isinstance(config, AttractivePot):         # RTIP Gaussian attraction
if isinstance(config, SynthesisPot):          # RTIP Gaussian synthesis
if isinstance(config, ReactionCoordinatePot): # RC-MD harmonic oscillator, JAX only
```

### Mechanism 1: RTIP Gaussian Bias (Ported from Rust, Exact Counterpart)

**Config**: `RepulsivePot` / `AttractivePot` / `SynthesisPot` ([bias.py:18-61](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/pes/bias.py#L18-L61))
**PES**: `Rtip0PES` ([core/rtip.py:196-260](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/core/rtip.py#L196-L260))
**Reaction coordinate**: RTI distance - RMSD after optimal rotational/translational alignment of two configurations (quaternion method, eigendecomposition of a 4x4 matrix and using the smallest eigenvalue)

Potential function ([core/rtip.py:159-167](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/core/rtip.py#L159-L167)):

```
pot = sum (w_i / sum w) * a * exp(-r_i^2 / 2 sigma^2)
w_i = 1 / r_i^7    # weight function; nearby configurations dominate
```

Parameter scheduling ([md.py:206](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/workflows/md.py#L206)):
```python
a_min = config.para.a0 * float(step)    # repulsive: positive amplitude grows linearly with step
a_min = -config.para.a0 * float(step)   # attractive: negative amplitude (attractive well deepens)
```

Core features:
- 1 reaction coordinate (RTI distance), 1 adjustable parameter (amplitude a, grows with time)
- The potential is Gaussian: force decreases again at long distance (there is an optimal-force region), and does not pull infinitely
- sigma is updated dynamically at each step = RTI distance from the current configuration to the reference configuration
- Three modes: repulsive (push out from minimum), attractive (pull toward target), synthesis (multi-molecule assembly)
- Supports `atom_add_pot`: apply bias only to selected atoms ([system.py](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/system.py))

### Mechanism 2: RC-MD Harmonic Restraint (JAX Only, No Rust Counterpart)

**Config**: `ReactionCoordinatePot` ([bias.py:151-170](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/pes/bias.py#L151-L170))
**PES**: `ReactionCoordinatePES` ([bias.py:105-148](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/pes/bias.py#L105-L148))
**Reaction coordinate**: two specific interatomic distances (B-X and N-C)

Key point: `_reaction_coordinate_md_bias` ([md.py:248-251](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/workflows/md.py#L248-L251)) **ignores the step parameter**, so k is always constant.

Core features:
- 2 distance coordinates, 1 shared k, fixed target
- The potential is harmonic: the farther away, the larger the force (-> inevitably causes temperature runaway)
- Attractive mode only (target = distances from reference product geometry)
- Automatic atom-index detection (`detect_reaction_config()` in rcmd_lib.py)

### Why It Is Called `rti_dist`

The MD output format was designed for RTIP Gaussian bias ([md.py:170](rtipmd/jax/src/rtip_jax/workflows/md.py#L170)), with the column name hard-coded as `rti_dist`. When RC-MD reuses the format, `_reaction_coordinate_md_bias` inserts `bias_pes.distance_error(system)` (the RMS of the two harmonic deviations) into the same column position. Therefore the "rti_dist" output in RC-MD is actually `sqrt((d_BX-target_BX)^2 + (d_NC-target_NC)^2)`.

### Correction to the Name "1D Restraint"

The RC-MD "1D" restraint should be described more accurately as a **"single-k, 2-distance harmonic restraint"**. The Rust/JAX RTIP Gaussian bias is the truly **"1D"** mechanism (1 RTI distance, 1 growing parameter a). But the weakness of RTIP Gaussian bias is that it does not know "which bond has formed" - the entire reaction progress is compressed into one scalar and cannot separately observe B-X and N-C bond formation the way RC-MD can.

## Why RTIP Gaussian Bias Can Drive Small-Molecule Association Reactions

### Adaptive Sigma Is the Key Design

There is an important correction to the previous analysis: RTIP Gaussian bias does **not** have a fixed width. sigma is set at each step to the current RTI distance from the system to the reference configuration:

```python
# md.py:218 (_attractive_md_bias) / md.py:235 (_synthesis_md_bias)
sigma_min = rti_dist(_coords(config.final_state, indices), _coords(system, indices))
```

This means that in the Gaussian function `a * exp(-r^2/2 sigma^2)`, r ~= sigma (the first RTI distance equals sigma), so:

```
exp(-r^2/2 sigma^2) = exp(-sigma^2/2 sigma^2) = exp(-1/2) ~= 0.607, constant!
```

**The exponential term never decays.** No matter how far the fragments are from the reference point, the exponential part of the Gaussian is always the constant 0.607.

Main force term (ignoring weight redistribution):
```
F proportional to a / sigma^2 * exp(-1/2) = a0*step / r^2 * 0.607
```

- Force decays as **~1/r^2** (a mild inverse-square decay), not exponential decay
- a grows linearly with step -> force is continuously compensated over time
- The system cannot "outrun" the bias, because sigma adaptively tracks the distance (ratchet effect)
- For fixed-sigma Gaussian, force -> 0 far away; for adaptive-sigma RTIP, far-field force -> `a0*step/r^2`

### Fixed Sigma vs Adaptive Sigma

| | Fixed sigma Gaussian | RTIP adaptive sigma (sigma=r) |
|---|---|---|
| exponential term | `exp(-r^2/2 sigma^2)` -> 0 (far away) | `exp(-1/2)` = 0.607 constant |
| force decay | exponential collapse | ~1/r^2 (mild) |
| far-field behavior | force vanishes, cannot pull | force is weak but present, continuously pushes outward |
| with step-growing a | - | force is linearly compensated over time |

### Why Repulsive Can Also Be Used for Association Reactions

The original Rust MD layer has only `impl RtipNVTMD for RepulsivePot` ([md.rs:18](rtipmd/rust/src/pes_exploration/md.rs#L18)), but the design philosophy of the framework is "escape from a local minimum" ([lib_2mol.rs:1-6](rtipmd/rust/src/lib_2mol.rs#L1-L6)):

> RTIP is a biased potential to drive the molecule escaping from local minimum

For association reactions, **the isolated monomer state is itself a local minimum**. Repulsive bias pushes the system out of the monomer state -> the system must explore non-monomer configurations -> these include dimer/oligomer/polymer states. Escaping the monomer basin is itself association.

In MD, thermal fluctuations help: repulsive bias provides a persistent outward "stirring force", while thermal motion provides exploration in orthogonal directions, forming a **biased random walk** (similar to metadynamics, but with a different bias form). Adaptive sigma ensures force never disappears, and step growth ensures force keeps increasing, producing a ratchet effect - once the system leaves the monomer state, bias pushes it farther away.

`atom_add_pot` ([md.rs:38-65](rtipmd/rust/src/pes_exploration/md.rs#L38-L65)) allows applying bias only to reaction-site atoms, making the RTI distance more directly correspond to the reaction coordinate and ignoring spectator-atom noise.

### Pathway Layer vs MD Layer

The Rust trait system ([traits.rs](rtipmd/rust/src/pes_exploration/traits.rs)) defines them as independent interfaces:

```rust
pub trait RtipPathSampling { ... }  // Pathway interface
pub trait RtipNVTMD { ... }         // MD interface
```

| | Pathway layer | MD layer |
|---|---|---|
| **temperature** | 0 K (`temp=nan`, `kin=nan`) | finite temperature, Berendsen thermostat |
| **motion** | 1D line search along total force direction (golden-section), direct displacement | leapfrog integration of coordinates + velocities, Berendsen thermostat temperature control |
| **velocity** | no velocity concept | has velocities, Maxwell-distributed |
| **stop criterion** | state machine: pot_drop disables bias -> f_epsilon convergence stops | fixed steps, runs to max_step |
| **goal** | find a minimum-energy path (IRC/GSM-like) | simulate finite-temperature real dynamics trajectory |
| **randomness** | only initial perturbation, then deterministic line search | thermal fluctuations throughout, random walk |
| **Rust implementation** | Repulsive/Attractive/Synthesis all exist | **Repulsive only** |

The division in the original Rust design:
1. **Pathway layer**: use AttractivePot or SynthesisPot to find the association path (fragments -> product; all 3 bias types exist)
2. **MD layer**: perform finite-temperature sampling/relaxation from the path start; repulsive bias prevents falling back into the reactant basin

The MD layer does not need Attractive/Synthesis because "where to go" has already been solved in the Pathway layer. MD only answers "how does it move at finite temperature."

### Why the MD Layer Had to Be Extended for ic5c02384

For N2 + small-molecule coupling reactions:
- The fragments start far apart, and the 0 K line search in Pathway (pure force-driven, no thermal assistance) cannot find a reasonable reaction path
- MD itself must "drive" the reaction - it must not only sample, but actively pull fragments together
- Repulsive can only push away from monomers and cannot specify "which two atoms should form bonds"
- -> Attractive/Synthesis/RC-MD must be added at the MD layer

This is what week1conclusion.md means by: "Attractive / synthesis MD and ReactionCoordinate were added on the JAX side during this study after finding that repulsive does not work for association reactions."

### RC-MD vs RTIP Gaussian: Suitable Scenarios

| | RTIP Gaussian (Attractive/Synthesis) | RC-MD harmonic oscillator |
|---|---|---|
| force vs distance | ~1/r^2 decay (weak but present far away) | ~r linear growth (farther means stronger) |
| suitable scenario | general small-molecule docking/association (no precise bond-length target) | association reactions with known specific bond-forming distances |
| convergence speed | slower, needs more steps | faster, but too-large k causes temperature runaway |
| interpretability | tune a0, physical meaning indirect | k is spring stiffness, physically direct |
| generality | high (does not rely on specific atom-pair knowledge) | low (requires knowing in advance which atom pairs form bonds and target distances) |

## 2026-05-26 Evening: JAX RTIP Gaussian Bias for 1-tBu__CO2 — Design Plan

### Background

All previous RC-MD experiments (Phase 1-3) used the harmonic restraint (`ReactionCoordinatePot`, `E = ½k(r-r₀)²`), which was added specifically for the ic5c02384 paper in the JAX layer and does not exist in the original Rust code. The user requests using the RTIP Gaussian bias form consistent with the original Rust version for the 1-tBu__CO2 reaction.

### RTIP Gaussian Bias Mathematical Form (Rust Original)

```
E_bias = Σ_i (w_i / Σw) * a * exp(-r_i² / 2σ²)

where:
  w_i = 1 / r_i⁷           # weight function, nearby configurations dominate
  a = a0 * step             # amplitude grows linearly with step (negative for attractive)
  σ = rti_dist(ref, cur)   # adaptive sigma = RTI distance from current to reference
```

Key characteristics:
- **Adaptive sigma**: `σ = rti_dist(ref, cur)`, making `exp(-r²/2σ²) ≈ exp(-1/2) ≈ 0.607` constant
- **Force decay ~1/r²**: gentle inverse-square, not exponential collapse
- **Ratchet effect**: sigma adaptively tracks distance, system cannot "outrun" the bias
- **1 reaction coordinate** (RTI distance) + 1 tunable parameter (a0), truly "1D"

### Comparison with RC-MD Harmonic Restraint

| | RTIP Gaussian (Rust original) | RC-MD Harmonic (previous exps) |
|---|---|---|
| Potential | `a·exp(-r²/2σ²)` | `½k(r-r₀)²` |
| Reaction coord | 1 RTI distance (global RMSD) | 2 interatomic distances (B-X, N-C) |
| Tunable param | a0 (amplitude growth rate) | k (force constant, fixed) |
| Force-distance | ~1/r² decay (weak but persistent at distance) | ~r linear growth (stronger at distance → T runaway) |
| Sigma | Adaptive (= RTI distance) | N/A |
| Param schedule | a grows linearly with step | k constant throughout |
| Far-field behavior | Force weak but present, sustained pull/push | Force diverges → inevitable T runaway |
| Rust counterpart | **Exists** (rtip.rs + md.rs) | **Does not exist** (JAX only) |

### Problems Identified

#### Problem 1: CLI Does Not Support Attractive/Synthesis MD

`cli.py`'s `deepmd-pathway` and `deepmd-md` subcommands hardcode `RepulsivePot`. Attractive MD cannot be run via CLI and requires a Python API script.

#### Problem 2: Rust Original Only Has Repulsive MD

Rust `md.rs` only implements `impl RtipNVTMD for RepulsivePot`. Attractive MD and Synthesis MD are JAX-side extensions. However, "consistent with Rust form" refers to the **bias functional form** (Gaussian + adaptive sigma). JAX's `AttractivePot` MD fully satisfies this (only `a = -a0 * step`, everything else identical).

#### Problem 3: Bias Reference Configuration Choice

- **Repulsive mode**: `local_min` = IS separated configuration, push away from reactant basin → explore bound states
- **Attractive mode**: `final_state` = product.xyz, pull toward known product
- Both modes share the same mathematical form; the physical meaning is inverted. For a reaction with known product, Attractive is more direct.

#### Problem 4: `atom_add_pot` — Include or Not

RTI distance computes global configurational RMSD. With all 36 atoms, thermal fluctuations of spectator atoms (H on tBu) dilute the reaction site signal. Rust supports `atom_add_pot` for selective biasing. For initial baseline, use all-atom RTI.

#### Problem 5: Initial Velocity Strategy

- Rust original: zero initial velocity, coordinate perturbation only (`scale=0.1`)
- Existing research script: directed initial velocity toward target (`_target_velocity()`)
- For Rust consistency, choose zero initial velocity

#### Problem 6: Parameter `a0` Selection

- Rust default: `a0 = 0.002`
- Research script: `a0 = 0.0005`
- No prior knowledge; requires scanning

#### Problem 7: SynthesisPot vs AttractivePot

- **AttractivePot**: `final_state` = product.xyz, RTI distance includes product bonding info
- **SynthesisPot**: dynamically computes geometric-center aggregation, no bond-length info
- For reactions with known product: AttractivePot is more appropriate

#### Problem 8: Pathway + MD Combination

Rust design philosophy: Pathway (0K line search) → MD (finite-T sampling). But JAX's AttractivePathway and AttractiveMD are independent. For first run, use MD directly; consider Pathway → MD pipeline later.

#### Problem 9: Runtime Environment

Must run on n5 server (DeePMD-kit 3.1.1 + CUDA 12.9.1 + DPA-3.2-5M.pt). Cannot run DeePMD locally.

### Analysis: Why RTIP Underperformed RC-MD on 1-tBu__CO2

**RTIP Attractive bias (a0=0.002, 1000 steps) did not drive 1-tBu__CO2 to product. The RC-MD harmonic restraint (k=0.006, 1000 steps) remains the only successful method for this reaction.**

Three root causes:

**1. RTI distance is a "blunt" reaction coordinate**

RC-MD directly restrains the two specific interatomic distances that define the reaction (B-O and N-C). RTIP uses the global configurational RMSD over all atoms — it knows the CO2 fragment should be near the tBu fragment, but doesn't know which atoms should form bonds. The system can reduce RTI distance by moving CO2 generally closer without specifically forming B-O and N-C bonds.

Evidence: `product_core_rmsd = 1.915 A` (core atoms roughly near product positions) but `B-O = 3.144 A` and `N-C = 4.007 A` (specific bonds not formed).

**2. Temperature still rises despite adaptive sigma**

The design expectation was that adaptive sigma (~1/r² force decay) would prevent temperature runaway. In practice:
- Zero initial velocity → T=0.18K at step 1
- Thermostat tries to heat to 300K (lambda=1.14 at step 1)
- Bias continuously injects energy (a grows from 0 to -2.0)
- By step 1000, T=1163K — higher than RC-MD's 614K

The bias potential reaches -1.21 Ha (~33 eV), far exceeding chemical bond energies. The gentle ~1/r² decay helps at long range but the growing amplitude compensates, and the cumulative energy injection overwhelms the thermostat.

**3. 1000 steps insufficient for RTI-based convergence**

RTI distance decreased from 33.5 to 19.9 (40% reduction), indicating the bias IS pulling the fragments together. But the convergence rate is much slower than RC-MD (which went from 7.97 to 0.34). The RTI distance metric is inherently less sensitive to the specific bond-forming coordinate, so it requires more steps.

### Comparison Summary

| Aspect | RC-MD harmonic | RTIP Gaussian |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Reaction coordinate | 2 specific distances | 1 global RMSD |
| Target knowledge | Uses product bond lengths | Uses product full geometry |
| 1-tBu__CO2 result | **Success** (B-O=1.44, N-C=1.66) | Failure (B-O=3.14, N-C=4.01) |
| Temperature control | 614K | 1163K (worse) |
| Convergence speed | Fast (0.34 rti_dist) | Slow (19.9 rti_dist) |
| Generality | Low (needs known product) | High (any reference geometry) |

### Conclusion

For reactions where the product structure is known and specific bond-forming distances can be identified, **RC-MD harmonic restraint is strictly superior to RTIP Attractive bias.** RTIP's advantage — generality without needing atom-pair knowledge — becomes a disadvantage when that knowledge is available.

RTIP Gaussian bias is more suitable for:
- Reactions where the product structure is unknown
- General small-molecule docking without specific bond-length targets
- Repulsive mode (pushing away from reactant basin to explore bound states)
- Synthesis mode (coalescing multiple fragments without bond-specific targets)
